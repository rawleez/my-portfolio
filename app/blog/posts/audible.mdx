### Problem

Audible treats audiobooks primarily as a linear listening experience rather than a medium for active reading and learning. Users can't quickly navigate to specific examples, subsection headings, glossary terms, names, or memorable passages within a chapter, making it difficult to use audiobooks for study, reference, or revisiting key ideas.

### Hypothesis

If listening to an audiobook is truly equivalent to reading a book as a way of learning, then audiobook listeners should have access to the same navigation, search, and reference tools that readers have. Audio learning is not inherently passive; many listeners actively study, review concepts, and revisit information. Therefore, audiobook experiences should support the same workflows as ebooks rather than assuming users only consume content linearly.

### Solution

Bring feature parity between Audible and ebook platforms by enabling transcript-powered search and subsection navigation. Automatically identify subsection headings using the table of contents and transcript, then make them searchable alongside keywords, phrases, names, glossary entries, and references.

### Action

- Add full transcript indexing for word and phrase search.
- Align audiobook transcripts with the table of contents to create subsection markers.
- Detect spoken headings and generate navigation points within chapters.
- Surface search results with timestamps that jump directly to the relevant point in the audio.
- Expand Audible's reading tools to match Kindle and Apple Books, enabling active learning workflows for audiobook listeners.

### Result

Audiobooks become a true learning medium rather than just a listening medium. Users can efficiently study, reference, and revisit information just as they would in a physical or digital book. If Audible embraces the idea that listening is a valid form of reading, this feature delivers the tools necessary to make that philosophy real.
